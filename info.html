There are three things to consider in order to understand gentrification:

  • The historic conditions, especially policies and practices that made communities susceptible to gentrification.
  • The way that central city disinvestment and investment patterns are taking place today as a result of these conditions.
  • The ways that gentrification impacts communities.

Historic Conditions

Historic conditions that have led to gentrification include:

  • Redlining: Standards set by the federal government and carried out by banks (1930s-1960s) explicitly labeled neighborhoods home to predominantly people of color as "risky" and "unfit for investment." This meant that many people of color were unable to access loans that would have allowed them to buy or repair homes in their own neighborhoods.
  • White flight: Housing and transportation policies of the mid-20th century, such as the GI Bill, fueled the growth of mostly white suburbs. The GI Bill promised low-cost mortgage loans for WWII soliders. However, due to discrimination, Black veterans were often unable to purchase homes in the suburbs.
  • Urban renewal: The highway system expansion often occurred in low-income neighborhoods, which resulted in the mass clearance of homes, businesses, and neighborhood institutions.
  • Foreclosure crisis: The crisis left low-income neighborhoods vulnerable to investors due to mass forclosures in those areas.

Central city disinvestment and investment patterns

People are going back to historically disinvested neighborhoods because of:

  • Relative affordability
  • Older, historic housing stock that appeals to new residents
  • Close proximity to city centers, and in turn jobs, restaurants, and transportation
  • Revitalization: cities are investing in the infrastructure and transit systems of some neighborhoods in order to appeal to newcomers

Impact of gentrification on communities

Gentrification is usually associated with displacement, which means that in some communities, long-term residents can't afford to stay to benefit from the neighborhoods' new investments.

Another type of displacement is cultural displacement, in which changes in the make-up of a neighborhood leads to a resident feeling out of place in their own home.
